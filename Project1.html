From bricklayers to waiters: labor market adjustment of Spanish workers during The Great Recession

This paper analyzes the employment contraction effect in the Spanish construction sector on workers’ employment and earning trajectories from 2007 to 2012. Moreover, it quantifies the relevance of the interaction between worker skills and local labour demand on the worker’s labour market adjustment during the same period. The identification strategy exploits variation in the construction sector’s employment contraction across Spanish provinces and a rich set of worker characteristics available in administrative panel data (MCVL). I show that individuals initially employed in the construction sector experienced a large impact on their cumulative wages during Spain’s economic downturn. An increase of one standard deviation in the local shock is associated with a decrease in cumulative earnings equivalent to four monthly wages over the 2007-2012 period. This impact resulted from longer unemployment and non-employment spells rather than a decrease in the average wages of exposed workers. Supported on the previous results, I study to what extent the local sectoral composition contributes to alleviating the shock’s impact on worker’s outcomes. To study this systematically, I construct a measure that captures the likelihood of transitioning from the construction sector to any other sector (reallocation index), conditional on the worker’s characteristics and the pre-shock local sectoral composition. The results suggest that earnings losses are larger for workers in provinces where the sectoral composition provides fewer opportunities to change sectors. Conditional on the increase of one standard deviation on the shock, an increase of one standard deviation of the reallocation index decreases the shock’s impact by 17%. Furthermore, the reallocation probabilities depend on the interaction of worker and regional characteristics. One implication of these findings is that high sectoral concentrations in a region may harm worker’s outcomes in case of a sectoral shock. Finally, these findings support that short term labour market adjustments are mainly intersectoral rather than inter-regional, consistent with recent empirical evidence in other settings..
